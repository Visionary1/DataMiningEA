Executive summary

We are going to use data set from Kaggle, New Car Sales in Norway (2007~2017 Jan) to predict future(2017 Feb~) car sales in Norway. The forecasting process will be done in pure R, especially using forecast package. On 1st scrum, we used ARIMA to do the forecast, as professor suggested that ARIMA is good for dealing with time series data. On 2nd scrum, we are going to run two additional models besides ARIMA to find out the fittest model. Rather than starting a prediction right away, we will first run models to predict sales in 2016 to check how precise they are. We will see which model gives the least errors compared to actual data. And then we will use the best model(which gives the least error) to predict sales in 2017.

Problem Description

We want to be able to predict future data results based on what already achieved in the past, without guessing wildly, where everyone is able to make speculation backed by data. With the available past sales data’s range of 10 years, we can use it to predict the sales data for following months. As of now, our available sales data up to January 2017, and it gives us opportunity to attempt predicting sales for February, March, May, and so on. The problem with predicting the future is uncertainties. If we ignore this problem, it will makes the whole project itself no different than making a wild guess. Uncertainties happened when the data that was provided shows more than one possible result, which means the further we try to predict, the more uncertainties shows up as we still need to determine the amount of possible outcome from the earlier prediction.
The forecasting models will greatly help us in predicting the future based on our available data and it match well to assist us in processing.

Utility

The information that is derived from forecasting analysis can be applied in various terms and these insights might be useful especially for car manufacturers in XXI century’s competitive business environment. Since Norway is ecofriendly country, the current trend on electric and hybrid car market gives a challenge for companies to find more competitive ways for selling their cars to Norwegian people. By knowing sales forecast, car manufacturers can estimate their expenses on manufacturing, distribution, servicing, and others. Also, it might help them to adjust their manufacturing facility to meet with the future demand. This is like a chain system, by knowing forecast, it will help companies to work more efficiently, not face with cash availability problems, make effective marketing strategy, predict competitor’s reaction, and have better measures of sales potential, like how much will be the maximum total revenue or number of units that company will sell.

Choosing a model

We did mention that we would use several models to see how they predict sales in 2016. We will use the model that go close to the actual data for forecasting of 2017. As we are asked to use 3 different models(1 for 1st scrum, +2 for 2nd scrum), we will use the following models.

  1. Exponential State Smoothing(ETS)
  2. TBATS
  3. ARIMA (did this in 1st scrum)

Model 1. Exponential State Smoothing(ETS)

The ETS function is in forecast package, and is used for making forecasts. Although it is best used in data that where past observations are weighted equally(simple moving average), we did give it a try.

fitstl<-stl(dt_ts, s.window = "period")
m_ets<-forecast(fitstl, method = "ets", h=12)
plot(m_ets)

By specifying h=12, we made a prediction for next 12 months.
The gap between gray area and blue area(AKA confidence interval) seems big. We’ll compare this prediction with other models and with actual data later.

Model 2. TBATS

TBATS model is designed for forecasting and is best applied when the data has cyclic patterns.

fut_tbats = forecast(tbats(dt_ts), h=12)
plot(fut_tbats)

Prediction seems almost same as Model 1.

Model 3. ARIMA

ARIMA is considered as the best function for predicting time series. auto.arima() automatically optimizes parameter and gives you the best model(within ARIMA).
On 1st scrum, we wrote ARIMA manually, but, turned out on 2nd scrum that it could be done automatically by using auto.arima.

1st scrum (legacy way)

library(tseries)

adf.test(diff(log(dt_ts)), alternative="stationary", k=0)
(fit <- arima(log(dt_ts), c(0, 1, 2),seasonal = list(order = c(1, 0, 0), period = 12)))
pred <- predict(fit, n.ahead = 1*12)
ts.plot(dt_ts,2.718^pred$pred, log = "y", lty = c(1,3))

2nd scrum (using auto.arima)

aarima = auto.arima(dt_ts)
fut_aa = forecast(aarima, h=12)
plot(fut_aa)

Both way produce a same result.
